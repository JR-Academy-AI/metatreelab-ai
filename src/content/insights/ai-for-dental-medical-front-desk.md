The average four-chair dental practice fielding 150 calls a week is paying someone $55,000–$65,000 a year to answer the phone. About 70% of those calls are booking requests, confirmations, or questions with single-word answers: "Yes, we take HCF." "Parking's on the left." "Your appointment is Tuesday at 2." Genuinely useful work that does not require a trained human brain.

AI front-desk tools designed for healthcare are now good enough to handle most of it. Voice AI no longer sounds robotic, appointment-booking integrations talk directly to PMS systems like Dental4Web, Exact, and Best Practice in real time, and patient intake can happen via text before the patient ever walks in. The question is where to start.

## Where the time actually goes

Track your front desk for one week. In most GP or dental practices, inbound calls break down like this: 40–50% are appointment bookings or reschedules; 20–25% are confirmations or reminder callbacks; 10–15% are billing and insurance questions; the rest is directions, after-hours voicemails, and results inquiries. The first 70% is almost entirely automatable today.

The other drain is outbound: manually calling patients to fill cancelled slots, sending hygiene recall notices three months past due, and chasing incomplete forms the morning of an appointment. Most practices still do this semi-manually. They shouldn't.

## The no-show problem is worth more than you think

No-show rates in dental and general practice typically run 10–15% without active outreach. A three-touch automated reminder sequence — 72 hours out by SMS, 24 hours out by SMS, same-day voice call — consistently brings that down to 3–6%. For a practice with 80 appointments a week at $220 average revenue per slot, dropping no-shows from 12% to 4% recovers roughly **$73,000 in annual revenue** that was simply evaporating.

That's not a cost-saving story. It's a revenue story. And the tooling costs $200–500 a month.

## After-hours: the gap most practices are ignoring

Most practices miss 20–30% of their inbound calls. Some go to voicemail; many happen after 5pm when the front desk has gone home. A patient who gets a voicemail will often try the practice down the street before calling back. AI voice agents can handle after-hours booking and urgent-versus-routine triage 24/7 — routing genuine emergencies to an on-call number and scheduling everything else for the next morning.

## What AI won't fix

Clinical note documentation is a separate category worth mentioning. Ambient AI tools like Heidi Health, Suki, or Nuance DAX can draft structured clinical notes from consultation audio, cutting documentation time by 30–50% for GPs and specialists. Genuinely useful — but it's a clinician tool, not a front-desk tool, and it requires a careful review of your clinical software's compliance position before deployment.

More importantly: don't automate your way out of human connection. Anxious new patients, chronic-condition check-ins, and any conversation touching a serious diagnosis need a real person. The practices using AI well use it to protect their staff's time for those interactions — not to replace them.

## Where to start

If you haven't touched any of this yet, start with automated appointment reminders. Lowest risk, highest return: one or two hours of setup, $100–300 a month in tooling, and a measurable drop in no-shows within 30 days. Add AI inbound call handling second once you've confirmed your PMS integration works. After-hours coverage and recall automation come next.

Custom AI builds — voice agents scripted specifically to your practice's workflow — make sense once you've validated the category with an off-the-shelf tool and found a gap it can't fill.
